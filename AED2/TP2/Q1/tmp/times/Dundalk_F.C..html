Dundalk Football Club (/dʌnˈdɔːk/; Irish: Cumann Peile Dhún Dealgan) is a professional association football club in Dundalk, Ireland. Dundalk compete in the League of Ireland Premier Division, the top tier of Irish football, and are the League Champions and FAI Cup holders, having won the Double in 2018. Founded in 1903 as the works-team of the Great Northern Railway, they entered the League in 1926 and became the first club outside of Dublin to win the title in 1932–33. They have subsequently won honours in each of the last nine decades to become the second most successful club in the League's history, and the most successful in the Premier Division era.

Dundalk first entered European competition as League Champions in 1963–64, and play their 70th European match in the 2019–20 Champions League. They became the first Irish club to win an away match in Europe in 1963–64, and were also the first Irish club to win points in the Group Stages of European competition in 2016. They are now the highest ranked Irish side in European football as measured by UEFA club coefficients.[1]

The club's traditional colours of white shirts with black shorts and trim were adopted in 1940, leading to the nickname "The Lilywhites". Supporters also refer to Dundalk as "The Town". Their home ground has been Oriel Park on the Carrickmacross Road since 1936,[2] and their core support has historically been drawn from the area now comprising the Dundalk Municipal District, and its mid-Louth, South Armagh, East Monaghan hinterland.[3] Dundalk were taken into new ownership by a consortium of investors backed by Peak6 Investments in January 2018.[4] The club’s official sponsors are Fyffes, and the team's kit supplier is CX+ Sport.

History[edit]

Main article: History of Dundalk F.C.

Origins (1885–1925)[edit]

Dundalk G.N.R. 1923–24

The Dundalk G.N.R. Football Club was established around 1885,[5] initially as a rugby football club. In September 1903 it switched codes to association football,[6] thus setting in motion its journey to becoming the modern day Dundalk Football Club. At first the club played challenge matches against other clubs in the area, including sides from British Army regiments garrisoned in the town, then joined the original Dundalk & District League (DDL) in 1906. There are no records of the club being active between 1907–08 and 1912–13, however for 1913–14 it rejoined the DDL. The local league was dormant again during the First World War, but Dundalk G.N.R. did compete in both the Irish Junior Cup and Leinster Junior Cup competitions of 1913–14, 1914–15 and 1916–17.[7] The club appears to have been inactive again in the 1917–18 and 1918–19 seasons due to the war, but it re-formed for the 1919–20 season, affiliated with the Leinster Football Association, and joined both the revived DDL[8] and the Newry and District League. After reaching a Leinster Junior Cup final and dominating the local scene for three seasons, Dundalk G.N.R. joined the Leinster Senior League for the 1922–23 season.[9] The club played four seasons at Leinster Senior League level, before being elected to the League of Ireland on 15 June 1926 to replace Pioneers.[10]

League of Ireland early years (1926–1965)[edit]

On 21 August 1926 the team, still known as Dundalk G.N.R. and under the management of Joe McCleery, travelled to Cork to face Fordsons in the opening match of the 1926–27 season,[11] going on to finish in eighth position in their debut season. In order to grow the club beyond the restrictions imposed by being a works-team, its management committee decided to make it independent of the Great Northern Railway company, and by the end of the 1928–29 season the club's "G.N.R." moniker had been dropped (that season's Leinster Senior Cup Final being the last occasion the club competed as "Dundalk G.N.R." formally).[12] The club was converted to a membership-based limited company – "Dundalk Association Football Club Limited" – on 25 January 1932.[13][14]

Playing their home matches at the Athletic Grounds near the centre of the town, and with English manager Steve Wright "doing everything except selling the programmes",[15] Dundalk became the first team outside of Dublin to win the League in 1932–33.[16] Indeed, Dundalk were the first team outside Dublin or Belfast to win any national League title in Ireland since the inception of the original Irish League in 1890. Due to the Athletic Grounds being a municipal facility shared with other sports, the club decided to move permanently to land made available on the Carrick Road in 1936, naming the new ground "Oriel Park".[2] Dundalk would not win the title again for 30 years, and finding the resources to sustain a League challenge was always difficult in the early decades. However there was success in the cup competitions.[17]

The club won its first Dublin City Cup in the 1937–38 season, defeating Cork in the final,[18] then, after three previous FAI Cup Final losses, finally won its first FAI Cup in 1942 by defeating Cork United in the final in Dalymount Park.[19] This victory ended what supporters had come to see as a jinx – that Dundalk would never win a major Cup, given the three lost previous finals (as well as five Leinster Senior Cup final defeats) since Dundalk had joined the League. Five weeks later Dundalk were unofficially crowned "Champions of All Ireland", after winning the final of the inaugural Dublin and Belfast Inter-City Cup.[20] The following September, in the new season, a second Dublin City Cup was added.[21] Both the Dublin City Cup and the FAI Cup were won again in 1948–49,[22] the latter by beating Shelbourne in the Final,[23] to close out the 1940s.

The 1950s were a lean time for the club in the League. Player sales to English clubs following World War 2 had bankrolled the club in the late 1940s,[24] but these sales tailed off after the departure of Club Secretary Sam Prole to Drumcondra.[25] The management committee had tried to save money on expensive signings by focusing on improved coaching and playing standards, and hired five coaches from England between 1947 and 1955.[26] But none worked out and, struggling to make ends meet, as running costs were not met by gate receipts, Dundalk were regularly at or near the bottom of the table. But the club finally won the Leinster Senior Cup in 1950–51,[27] then went on to win the FAI Cup twice more during the decade – defeating Cork Athletic in the 1952 final,[28] and Shamrock Rovers in the 1958 final.[29]

At the start of 1960 Dundalk were top of the League,[30] and though the side fell away that season, it lead to optimism at the club that the lean years might be behind it. With trainer Gerry McCourt (a veteran of the 1932–33 League winning side), the club won its first League title for 30 years in 1962–63.[31] Following that success, Dundalk entered European competition for the first time, where they became the first Irish side to win an away leg of a European tie by beating FC Zurich 2–1 (in a 4–2 aggregate defeat) in the 1963-64 European Cup.[32] But within two seasons the team had fallen away again, and, with Oriel Park in need of significant investment, change was required.

Takeover and the Fox era (1966–1974)[edit]

Dundalk switched from being a membership-based company to a Public Limited Company in January 1966[33] and after the takeover the new board set about investing in both the team and Oriel Park, which consisted of turning the pitch 90 degrees, building a new stand and adding facilities.[34] In 1966–67, under new player/manager Alan Fox,[35] Dundalk finally won the club's first League of Ireland Shield after 40 years.[36] Fox's side followed up the Shield success by romping to the League title ahead of Bohemians by seven points[37] – a huge margin in the days of two points for a win and 22 games. This third League title brought the club's only League and Shield Double. To cap a memorable season Dundalk also won the Top Four Cup.[38]

Early the following season Oriel Park tasted European football for the first time, hosting Vasas SC of Hungary under newly installed floodlights.[39] The town swelled with pride at what its club was achieving. But a breakdown in relationships was to scupper the progress the club had made. Fox fell out with the board of directors during the trip for the return leg of the tie in Budapest, and was eventually released in March 1968.[40] Fox's final success at Oriel Park was the Dublin City Cup of 1967–68 – won that February.[41] 1968–69 started with a Fairs Cup win over DOS Utrecht.[42] but saw the club continue to drift in the League – a fourth-place finish and a Dublin City Cup (the club's fifth and last before the competition was discontinued)[43] being all that the remnants of Fox's team could muster.

Just like ten years earlier, Dundalk entered the new decade at the top of the League. Future Ireland manager Liam Tuohy had taken over in the summer of 1969 and had also joined the club's board.[44] Tuohy made an instant impression with his tactical and organisational skills. But due to the capital debt from the rebuild of Oriel Park and the spending under Alan Fox, Tuohy was obliged to thin the squad and slash the wage bill. While successful at blooding young players, it was too much to build a side that could sustain a title challenge and Tuohy eventually quit almost three years to the day after joining the club, criticising a lack of local support in the process.[45] In his three seasons Tuohy's sides won the 1970–71 Leinster Senior Cup[46] and the club's second and final Shield in 1971–72.[47]

Dundalk had to sell or release a number of players to survive after Tuohy left.[48] As a result, the club slid down the League, with a young, inexperienced squad finishing second from bottom in the table in 1972–73. The club's worst season since the 1950s brought the 'Fox era' to a close. To recover the situation a new board took over the running of the club and hired English player/manager John Smith from Walsall.[49] After renegotiating the club's debts, the new board were able to provide Smith with the funds to sign a number of players. There was a fast start to the new season, and a Leinster Senior Cup win over Bohemians in a replay[50] but Smith could only bring the club back to mid-table, and he quit two matches into his second season for a job outside football,[51] paving the way for the appointment of Jim McLaughlin.

Jim McLaughlin era (1975–1983)[edit]

Dundalk player-manager Jim McLaughlin in action away to PSV Eindhoven in 1976

It was under McLaughlin, appointed player/manager on 20 November 1974,[52] that Dundalk were brought to a new level of success. Dundalk cruised to McLaughlin's first League title (the club's fourth) in 1975–76,[53] and he followed that up with the club's first foray into Europe since 1969. In the European Cup Dundalk were drawn against PSV Eindhoven and were deemed unlucky to only draw the first leg in Oriel Park.[54] That match started an unbeaten run in Europe in Oriel Park of eight matches across the next five seasons. League form was mixed for the next two seasons, but with renewed focus another title followed in 1978–79.[55][56] Dundalk then defeated Waterford in the FAI Cup Final, thereby completing the club's first League and Cup Double.[57]

After two seasons as runners-up McLaughlin's final title at Dundalk arrived in 1981–82.[58] In addition to the Double, McLaughlin delivered two more FAI Cups – defeating Limerick United in the 1977 Final,[59] and Sligo Rovers in the 1981 Final.[60] To cap an unprecedented period of success in the club's history, McLaughlin's sides also won two Leinster Senior Cups[61][62], and two League Cups.[63][64] McLaughlin's Double winning side's 1979-80 European Cup run, where they narrowly missed out on qualifying for the Quarter-Finals (going down 3–2 on aggregate to Glasgow Celtic[65] in front of Oriel Park's record attendance), was the club's best ever European performance until 2016.[66] An end of season slump in the 1982–83 season, in which Dundalk slipped to third and missed out on Europe, signalled that the team was entering a transition period. But McLaughlin shocked Dundalk's board and fans by resigning in June 1983, saying he needed a change.[67][68]

McLaughlin's trophy haul while at Dundalk: three League titles, three FAI Cups, two League of Ireland Cups and two Leinster Senior Cups.

Turlough O'Connor era (1985–1993)[edit]

Former Dundalk player Turlough O'Connor was appointed as manager ahead of the League's split into two divisions in 1985–86.[69] He had to rebuild the team following the departure over the previous two years of almost all the players McLaughlin had left behind. But O'Connor quickly built a formidable squad of his own, and over the following eight seasons his Dundalk sides consistently finished in the Top Four. After winning the League Cup in 1986–87,[70] his first trophy as manager at the club, Dundalk finished as runners-up to Shamrock Rovers in both League and Cup, which brought European football back to Oriel Park for the first time since 1982. The following season started with a visit from Ajax,[71] and ended with the club's second League and Cup Double,[72] with the Cup being won against Derry City.[73]

O'Connor won his second League Cup in 1989–90,[74] and another League title followed in 1990–91 in an end of season, winner takes all match in Turner's Cross against Cork City.[75] But Dundalk spurned an opportunity to progress in the European Cup, when a 1–1 draw away to Honved was followed by a 0–2 home defeat. The squad was ageing, and the team's low-scoring, safety first playing style saw attendances drop as the team became less competitive. After losing the 1993 FAI Cup Final (in which nine of the squad were over 30), it was clear another rebuild was required. Dundalk started the 1993–94 season erratically – with thumping away victories being followed by defeats at home. After a home defeat to Monaghan United in October, during which he was abused by a section of the crowd, O'Connor resigned – a sour end to a successful reign.[76]

Turlough O'Connor left Dundalk with two League titles, one FAI Cup and two League Cups to his name.

Decline and relegation (1993–2002)[edit]

By the end of the 1992–93 season Dundalk Football Club was facing into severe headwinds – a new squad was required, Oriel Park needed upgrading, yet income was falling. A healthy surplus in 1989[77] became a serious deficit by 1993.[78] So a poor 1993–94 season happened at the worst possible time. Dundalk had moved quickly to replace Turlough O'Connor with another former player – Jim McLaughlin protege Dermot Keely,[79] who had won a League, two FAI Cups and one League Cup under McLaughlin at Dundalk. But a thin squad struggled and missed out on the "Top Six" round-robin format that decided the title, with the result that the club played out the final third of the season in the meaningless Bottom Six round-robin in a near deserted Oriel Park.

The 1994–95 season had barely started when the long-feared financial crisis came to a crunch that October. Unlike the capital debt crisis that caused the club's decline in the early '70s, the issue was now one of cashflow – matchday revenue and sponsorship were nowhere near enough to match the club's operating costs and it was effectively insolvent. Local businessmen formed a new Interim Company to take over the club (and its debts),[80] saving it from bankruptcy. The club had flirted with extinction and had a paper-thin squad, yet against that background Keely steered Dundalk to a ninth League title.[81][82] In 20 seasons between 1975 and 1995 Dundalk Football Club had won a combined total of 14 Leagues, FAI Cups and League Cups, and had competed regularly in Europe. But even the 1994–95 title success could not halt the decline.

Midway through the 1995–96 season Keely quit – frustrated at being unable to strengthen his squad.[83] A year later Dundalk were stuck in the bottom three and had to survive the end of season promotion/relegation play-off,[84] but it only prolonged the inevitable. The club turned to Jim McLaughlin to come out of retirement and try and turn things around.[85] But the financial issues reared their head again and, the night of a morale-boosting victory over defending champions St Patrick's Athletic at the end of November in the 1998–99 season, the whole squad was transfer listed to try and save money.[86] An end of season collapse saw the club drop from the top-tier for the first time since joining the League. Relegation was confirmed following a home defeat to UCD 20 years to the day after the club had won its first Double.[87]

With a supporters' Co-Op due to take over the club,[88] some optimism had returned and initial expectations were of an immediate return to the top flight. But the reality of life in the lower tier kicked in and a losing battle with the League and Kilkenny City over the latter playing an improperly registered player, which reached the High Court,[89] was one of the first hurdles to trip up the Co-Op. Under a new manager – former player Martin Murray – and with a lot of new players, the club was promoted as First Division Champions in 2000–01.[90] But the club was automatically relegated again the following season.[91] Nonetheless Dundalk picked themselves up to win a ninth FAI Cup a week later with victory over Bohemians in the Final.[92]

Promotion and upheaval (2006–2012)[edit]

After being relegated again Dundalk languished in the lower reaches of the First Division for the next four seasons.[93] However, after the club was taken back into private ownership under Gerry Matthews, who had bought the club's training ground in 2005 and later joined the board, Dundalk finished second under new manager John Gill in the 2006 season, securing a play-off tie against Waterford United. Despite winning the two-legged play-off,[94] Dundalk were still denied a place in the 2007 Premier Division, with Galway United, who had finished third in that season's First Division, selected by the FAI's 2006 IAG Report to be promoted ahead of Dundalk and Waterford.[95] In 2008 Dundalk finally won promotion back to the Premier Division, pipping Shelbourne to the top spot on the final night of the season.[96][97]

Controversially John Gill, the manager for the previous three seasons, was let go after winning the First Division title.[98] Back in the Premier Division Dundalk managed three mid-table finishes and qualified for the 2010–11 Europa League. But with a revolving door of managers, results deteriorating, and owner Gerry Matthews' decision to end his investment and exit the club,[99] the subsequent financial crisis during a disastrous 2012 season threatened to put the club out of existence altogether.[100] Matthews put the club up for sale[101] and, with the assistance of a Supporters Trust, the club was taken over by local businessmen Andy Connolly and Paul Brown (owners of official sponsors Fastfix Ltd), and Dundalk subsequently managed to remain in the top flight by beating Waterford United in the promotion/relegation play-off.[102]

Stephen Kenny's golden era (2013–2018)[edit]

Stephen Kenny, Dundalk manager 2013-2018

With the takeover complete and the club saved, the new owners turned to Stephen Kenny – out of work since being sacked by Shamrock Rovers – to become the new manager.[103] With a blank canvas, Kenny set about rebuilding the squad. When the 2013 season started neither supporters nor pundits were sure what to expect, and Dundalk failed to win any of the first five home matches. But with Kenny's team clicking into gear as his ideas took hold, they rose up the table to the most unlikely of title challenges, eventually finishing as runners-up.

Kenny kept the nucleus of the side together and made some more shrewd additions for the following season and went on to guide the club to its first League title since 1995.[104] Dundalk also won that season's League Cup,[105] the club's first League and League Cup Double. The 2015 season saw Dundalk cement their dominance of domestic football, cruising to the club's third League and FAI Cup Double,[106] three short years after a financial crisis that had threatened its existence. Dundalk went on to complete a Three-in-a-Row League title with two games to spare in 2016,[107] and made history the same season by being the first Irish club to gain a point,[108] and then to win a match,[109] in the Group Stages of European competition.

After the exertions of the Europa League run and the departure of some key players, Dundalk were runners-up in League and Cup in 2017, yet still won the League Cup.[110] But the team's European exploits had attracted interest from abroad, and a consortium of American investors, backed by sports-investors Peak6, completed a takeover of the club in January 2018.[4] Kenny's side reasserted itself as 'Ireland's Number One' in 2018, winning another League and Cup Double – the second under Kenny and fourth in the club's history – breaking points-total and goals scored-total records in the process.[111][112] To some shock but no surprise, Kenny resigned in November 2018 to become the Republic of Ireland U21 manager.[113]

Stephen Kenny's personal Roll of Honour at the club from 2013 to 2018: four League titles, two FAI Cups, two League of Ireland Cups and one Leinster Senior Cup.

As you were (2019–)[edit]

2019 squad warm-up before a match

In 2019 Dundalk were entering the club's 100th year of unbroken participation in League football – having joined the Dundalk & District League in 1919.[114] Hoping to achieve both stability and continuity, and with virtually the whole first-team squad signed to longer-term contracts, Dundalk replaced Kenny with Vinny Perth (his longtime Assistant Manager at Dundalk) as Head Coach, with John Gill returning to the club as First Team Coach.[115] The new management team tasted their first success when Dundalk won the President's Cup pre-season curtain raiser ahead of the 2019 season.[116] Despite a number of serious injuries to players (with all eight midfielders in the squad missing matches) and 13 points behind early leaders Shamrock Rovers in April, Dundalk headed into the mid-season break five points clear at the top of the table.[117]

Dundalk F.C. in European football[edit]

Dundalk's leading European goalscorer David McMillan in action in the 2016-17 Europa League.
Main article: Dundalk F.C. in European football

Dundalk first entered European competition in 1963-64 as League of Ireland Champions, became the first Irish club to win an away match in Europe the same season,[32] and were also the first Irish club to win points in the Group Stages of European competition in 2016. The club plays its 70th European match in the 2019-20 Champions League, having previously played against the likes of Liverpool, Tottenham Hotspur, Glasgow Celtic, FC Porto, PSV Eindhoven, Ajax, Red Star Belgrade, Hajduk Split, Legia Warsaw and Zenit St Petersburg.[118][119] In the UEFA Club Coefficients for 2018-19, Dundalk are ranked 155th on 7.000 points alongside Vitesse Arnhem and Panathinaikos,[1] making them the highest-ranked Irish club in Europe.

Colours and crest[edit]

Kit history[edit]

Dundalk's colours have been white shirts with black trim and black shorts and black or white socks since 1940. The club's original colours, when competing as Dundalk G.N.R., were black and amber-striped shirts with white shorts. In advance of dropping the 'G.N.R.' moniker, the club adopted a strip of white shirts, with a blue crest (representing the Coat of Arms of Dundalk), and navy-blue shorts. The new colours got their first run out in a match against Shamrock Rovers in January 1928.[120] White was chosen as when the clubs of the town first amalgamated as 'Dundalk Association Football Club' in 1904, they had also adopted white shirts.[121]

These colours were worn until 1939 but came to be seen as unlucky, due to the number of cup final losses racked up while wearing them.[122] Hoping a change of colours would bring more luck, the team wore a sky blue and maroon quartered shirt with white shorts and maroon socks in 1939–40. After that experiment the club returned to white shirts for the 1940–41 season, this time paired with black shorts and black socks. Possibly by coincidence, the amalgamated Dundalk A.F.C. – who became known as Dundalk Rovers – adopted black shorts to go with their white shirts in 1906, wearing the combination for the first time in an Irish Junior Cup tie with Dundalk G.N.R.[123] The first team 'Home' colours have remained essentially unchanged, although the club played in all-white in 1973–74 and 2003, and have also used all-white when required to avoid kit clashes.

Away colours

For most of the club's existence away colours were not required. When change colours were needed red shirts were used, and for the Cup Winners Cup tie away to Tottenham Hotspur in 1981 an all-red kit was worn. An all-red kit was also used in the 1990–91 League season. When regular away kits were introduced in 2000 the colours were typically variants on black or red with white trim.[124] Starting in 2012 other colour combinations have been used – for example yellow with blue sleeves, shorts and trim as a nod to new official sponsor Fyffes from 2012 to 2014; and black and gold in homage to the club's G.N.R. roots from 2016 to 2017.

Having previously only used ad hoc third colours when both home and away kits clashed with opponent's colours, Dundalk introduced a third strip for the first time in 2019 – an all-lilac strip with white and black trim designed by CX+ Sport, where the logo of the official sponsor Fyffes has been replaced by that of Temple Street Children's University Hospital, as part of a fundraising partnership between the club and the charity.[125]

Kit suppliers

The kit supplier from 2016 to 2019 was CX+ Sport (a Horseware Ireland company). Previous long-term kit suppliers have been O’Neills (1976–1984; 1990–2004) and Umbro (2007–2015). A Dundalk company, Eros Sportswear, supplied kits between 1985 and 1988, while Union Sport (1988–89), Erreà (2005) and Diadora (2006) have each been suppliers for one season.[126]

Crest history[edit]

The Coat of Arms of Dundalk ('three martlets proper on a blue field'), adopted for the crest in 1928, appears as the “Corporation Arms” in a town plan of Dundalk dating to 1675,[127] and is a simplification of the Seal of the New Town of Dundalk, which itself dates to the 14th Century.[128] The crest disappeared from the playing shirts by 1930 for unrecorded reasons, but is assumed by club historian Jim Murphy to have been a cost saving measure.[129] A modified crest of three martlets on a white shield with the club name was reintroduced to the shirt for the 1952 FAI Cup Final.[129] Following minor design changes over the years the white shield became a red shield with white martlets in 1997, as a nod to the town seal but in reversed tinctures. In 2015 this crest was modified to incorporate a gold star, to commemorate the club's tenth League of Ireland title.

Home grounds[edit]

Dundalk played at the Athletic Grounds in the centre of the town from 1903 until 1936, paying a ground fee per game. On occasions when the Athletic Grounds were unavailable, matches would usually move to the P.J. Carroll Company Recreation Ground or the grounds of St. Mary's College. The club secured its own ground on the Long Avenue in the town in the summer of 1921, however 12 months later the pavilion they had built was burned down at the height of the Irish Civil War (during which arson was rampant),[130] and the club returned to the Athletic Grounds.

Oriel Park[edit]

Main article: Oriel Park
Oriel Park, home of Dundalk Football Club

In 1936 the club moved permanently to "Casey's Field" on the Carrick Road on a long-term land lease, naming the new ground "Oriel Park".[2] Almost 10 years to the day after Dundalk G.N.R. played their first League of Ireland match away to Fordsons, the same club (as Cork F.C.) were the first visitors to the new ground, with the home team winning 2–1.[131] Oriel Park's attendance record is an estimated 17,000, set in 1979 for Dundalk's European Cup second round tie against Celtic.[66] On occasions when Oriel Park has been unavailable due to works on the ground, matches have been moved to United Park in Drogheda or Gortakeegan in Monaghan.

European matches[edit]

Main article: Dundalk F.C. in European football

Dundalk played their first ever home European match against F.C. Zurich in the 1963-64 European Cup in Dalymount Park in Dublin, as Oriel Park did not have floodlights.[132] Floodlighting was installed in Oriel for the visit of Vasas SC of Hungary four years later.[39] Dundalk had to move the Malmö tie in 1995 to United Park in Drogheda, due to the Oriel Park pitch being relaid, and the Varteks tie in 2002 to Tolka Park in Dublin, due to Oriel Park not meeting UEFA's upgraded standards for football stadiums.

Following ground improvements, Oriel Park is now a Category 2 Stadium able to accommodate 3,000 seated spectators for European games.[133] European matches requiring stadia to have a Category 3 status have been played in Tallaght Stadium owned by South Dublin County Council. Dundalk's Champions League Play-off round home leg against Legia Warsaw in 2016 required a stadium meeting Category 4 status and was played in the Aviva Stadium in Dublin.[134]

Supporters[edit]

Dundalk F.C.'s official Supporters Club was first formed as the ‘Dundalk Football Supporters’ League’ in 1937–38. The Supporters Club has, over many decades, raised vital funds in support of the football team.[77] Dundalk fans have nicknamed the club ”The Lilywhites” because of the white shirts worn by the team. Fans also refer to “The Town”, and use the hashtag #CmonTheTown on social media.[135] Previously the team were nicknamed "The Northerners" as a carryover from the club's original "G.N.R." (Great Northern Railway) moniker.[136] Other nicknames were "The Railwaymen",[137] and later "The Bordermen"[138] (due to the town's location close to the border with Northern Ireland).

During the 1980s and early 1990s the club's hardcore support congregated in Oriel Park's 'Shed', calling themselves the Mujahideen. A new generation of fans – who followed the club out of the First Division, through the ownership crisis of 2012, and into the club's successful 2014–2018 period – style themselves the 'Shedside Army'. The Shedside Army are responsible for Oriel Park's tifo displays. One such display – the flying of Palestinian flags in Oriel Park during a Europa League tie – resulted in a UEFA fine for the club of €18,000.[139]

Dundalk supporters have two mottoes: "We've Seen Things They'll Never See" owing to the roller-coaster of highs and lows the club has experienced;[140] and "Dundalk Will Never Die But You Will".[141] – a riff on a Mogwai album title. The club anthem has become Three Little Birds by Bob Marley and the Wailers (both for the obvious connotation with the club's crest, and the sentiments expressed in the lyrics).[142][143]

Support base and attendances[edit]

Town of Dundalk and geographic area of Dundalk F.C. core support

Dundalk Football Club's support base extends beyond the town of Dundalk itself in a radius of approx 30 km. It takes in the Dundalk Municipal District in north Louth, which comprises the towns of Dundalk, Carlingford and Blackrock; the Ardee Municipal District in mid-Louth, which comprises the towns of Ardee and Dunleer; the east Monaghan district of Castleblaney-Carrickmacross; and the south Armagh part of the Newry and Mourne district.[3] The total population of this area is just over 100,000.[144] Dundalk's average Friday night home League attendances have been consistent at approx 3,000 in the 2015–2019 period,[145] with attendances at 'bigger' matches of approx 4,000.[146][147]

Rivalries[edit]

The Louth Derby is contested between Dundalk and Drogheda United, who entered the League in 1963. The clubs played an annual friendly from 1966 to 1984 – the Donegan Cup,[148] presented by former Louth TD Paddy Donegan. Apart from one attempt to revive the contest in 1990,[149] it was discontinued as the two clubs could not find suitable dates for it during the season after the split of the League of Ireland into two divisions. The friendly was reintroduced as a pre-season match in 1997 with a new trophy – the Jim Malone Cup, in honour of three-time Chairman of Dundalk F.C., the late Jim Malone.[150]

While there is a sibling rivalry between the two towns,[151] League derbies are not usually fractious due to the clubs rarely competing for top honours simultaneously. Many of Dundalk's most successful periods have corresponded with Drogheda being at the lower end of the League or in the First Division, while Drogheda's main period of success (between 2004 and 2008) was achieved while Dundalk were in the lower tier. Nonetheless FAI Cup ties between the sides – there have been seven in all – have often been tempestuous affairs in front of large crowds,[152] with three going to replays.

In addition to the Louth Derby, Dundalk fans would see Shamrock Rovers as the club’s biggest rivals.[153] Rovers hold the record for the most League titles and the record for the most FAI Cups, with Dundalk next in the honours list for both competitions.

Ownership and finances[edit]

Dundalk Football Club is currently owned by a private company trading as "Dundalk Town FC Limited". The trading company has been owned by a consortium of U.S.-based investors backed by Peak6 Investments LLC following a buyout in January 2018.[4] These investors make up the club's board of directors (see Club officials section).[159]

Ownership history[edit]

As the association football club of the employees of the Great Northern Railway, Dundalk was 'owned' by the company and run by a management committee elected by the members of the club. The club was converted to a membership-based limited company – "Dundalk Association Football Club Limited" – on 25 January 1932,[160][14] effectively coming under the ownership of the club's supporters, who elected a management committee every two years. This ownership structure survived until the end of 1965 but by then Oriel Park was in need of investment, and ownership model, which saw the club break even on an annual basis at best, could not provide the finance required. As a result, the club was converted to a Public Limited Company – "Dundalk Football Club Limited" – in January 1966.[33]

The financial crisis that hit in late 1994, which saw the club become effectively insolvent, forced the liquidation of the 1966 company, and the club was taken over by "Dundalk AFC Interim Limited", made up of former and current directors under chairman Enda McGuill.[80] But the cash-flow problems that had faced the club through most of the 1990s remained, and crisis struck again in 1998.[86] The club was eventually taken over by the Dundalk F.C. Co-Operative in 2000,[88] returning the club to a supporter-owned, membership-based model. But the Co-Op was unable to make the sort of investment in either the team or Oriel Park required to bring the club back to the Premier Division,[161] and was left with little option but to sell the club's training ground, Hiney Park, in order to service debts and pay for work at Oriel Park.[162][163]

The man who purchased Hiney Park, Developer Gerry Matthews, joined the board as CEO in 2006,[164] then took the club back into private ownership as "Dundalk FC Limited" when it was accepted that the Co-Op ownership could not continue. Under the ownership of Matthews the club was returned to a somewhat solid footing, however his decision to end his financial support of the club lead to another crisis in 2012.[100] With the assistance of the Dundalk FC Supporters Trust, the club was rescued by the owners of official sponsor Fastfix Ltd, Paul Brown and Andy Connolly. They formed a new company "Dundalk Town FC Limited" who completed a takeover of the club in time for the 2013 season. Brown and Connolly then sold their interest in the club to the current owners in 2018.[4]

The current team shirt's chest logo is that of official sponsors Fyffes. Subsequently, the club had a number of official sponsors,[126] before the present partnership with Fyffes, which commenced in 2012.[166]

Player transfers

Players in the League of Ireland are typically signed on single season contracts, meaning they become free agents at the end of each season.[172] Contracts of two-year duration are less typical, and again such players are not usually transferred for a fee, with interested clubs waiting until the six-month pre-contract window to approach players directly; while players and clubs very rarely sign deals of a longer duration.[173] As a result the rapid inflation in transfer fees seen throughout European club football has not been a feature of the game in Ireland, and Dundalk have not benefited financially from player transfers since the Bosman ruling came into effect. Indeed, the club's PFAI Players' Player of the Year for 2015 (Richie Towell) and 2016 (Daryl Horgan) both transferred to EFL Championship clubs at the end of their respective award-winning seasons on free transfers due to contract expiry.[173]

Dundalk's transfer fees both paid and received have generally remained undisclosed. The record transfer fee received (when all clauses were eventually triggered and paid) is approx £80,000 (equivalent to €200,000 in 2019)[174] for Steve Staunton, who was signed by Liverpool in August 1986 for an initial fee of £20,000. Dundalk subsequently received a further estimated £70,000 (equivalent to €150,000 in 2019) when Staunton was transferred by Liverpool to Aston Villa in 1991.[175] The club's highest transfer fee received from another League of Ireland club was £25,000 from Shelbourne in 2000 for goalkeeper Steve Williams.[176] This equates to approx €43,000 in 2019.

Television

Ireland's State-owned public service broadcaster, RTÉ, has broadcast rights for League of Ireland and FAI Cup matches as part of a package from the FAI that includes international matches. However, there is little or no income derived from these rights for League of Ireland clubs.[177] Indeed, the network refused to pay the fee asked to broadcast Dundalk's Champions League tie home-leg against BATE Borisov in 2016[178] – a historic night for both the club and the League[179] – only a few months after Dundalk were named RTÉ’s ‘Team of the Year’ for 2015.[180] RTÉ had also previously offered the FAI €4 million to avoid having to televise any League of Ireland matches on its channels at all,[178] and has bounced its highlights show around the TV schedule. Concerns regarding RTÉ’s attitude to the Irish club scene go back as far as 1965, when former Dundalk Secretary Sam Prole was critical in an FAI Council meeting of the state-broadcaster's lack of support for domestic football.[181]

Commercial broadcaster Eir Sport televises a number of live League and European matches during the season, though, again, clubs receive little or no financial dividend, as the matches are considered a marketing opportunity for the League, rather than a commercial interest for the network.[177] However Dundalk's European run in 2016 was broadcast nearly in its entirety on Eir Sport.

Radio and online

Midway through the 2019 season, Dundalk announced a pilot project to stream League matches for viewers outside the Republic of Ireland on either a single match or four match subscription basis.[182] Due to broadcasting rights restrictions this service is not available to stream in the Republic of Ireland.

Highlights of all matches are made available by the club on its YouTube channel shortly after full-time, while live commentary of matches is broadcast on Dundalk FM (a community radio station) and LMFM. The radio broadcasts do not have licensing restrictions and can be accessed online in Ireland and globally from the stations' websites.

In 2015 the FAI agreed a deal with TrackChamp to stream all League of Ireland games outside Ireland.[183] Games are streamed via a single camera in relatively low resolution and without commentary. This service was due to be improved in time for the 2019 season.[184]

Records[edit]

Main article: List of Dundalk F.C. records and statistics

The record for the most appearances in all competitions is currently held by Tommy McConville who appeared in 580 matches for the club between 1964 and 1986.[188] Joey Donnelly is the club's top goalscorer with 142 goals in all competitions. Four other players – Eddie Carroll, Joe Martin, Paddy Turner and Jimmy Hasty – have also scored 100 goals or more for the club.[189]

Bob Egan became the first Dundalk player to win an international cap on 20 April 1929 when he represented Ireland in a 4–0 defeat of Belgium.[190] The player who has won the most caps as a Dundalk player is Billy O'Neill who won 11 caps for Ireland during his spell with the club, with Mick Fairclough being the most recently capped as a Dundalk player.

Dundalk's largest domestic victories have been 9–0 wins over Jacobs in 1932 and Shelbourne in 1980. The biggest victory in a European football match was a 4–0 home win against Fram Reykjavík in 1981.[191]
